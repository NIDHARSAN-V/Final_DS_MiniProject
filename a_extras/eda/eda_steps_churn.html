1
Target variable analysis
First thing — understand what you're predicting. Check how many customers churned vs renewed. If churn is only 10% of your data, that's a class imbalance problem you'll need to handle later.
value_counts() churn rate % pie / bar chart
In your dataset: Prospect_Outcome — how many "Churned" vs "Renewed"?
2
Univariate analysis — numerical columns
Look at each numerical feature individually. Understand the shape, spread, and any outliers. Are values normally distributed or skewed? Do extreme outliers exist?
histogram boxplot describe() skewness kurtosis
Key columns: Connection_Net, Tenure_Years, Total_Net_Paid, Discount_Amount, all score columns
3
Univariate analysis — categorical columns
For category-type columns, check frequency of each category. Are there rare categories with very few records? Do any categories dominate heavily?
bar chart value_counts() frequency table
Key columns: Band, Connection_Group, Tenure_Group, Renewal_Month, Proforma_Account_Stage
4
Bivariate analysis — feature vs churn (the most important step)
This is the heart of EDA for a churn model. Compare every feature between churned and non-churned customers. Which features look different between the two groups? Those are your strongest predictors.
grouped boxplot churn rate by category mean comparison stacked bar chart
e.g. Do churned customers have lower Tenure_Years? Higher Discount_Amount? Lower Auto_Renewal_Score?
5
Correlation analysis
Check how numerical features relate to each other. Highly correlated features (e.g. 0.9+) are redundant — you only need one of them. Also check which features correlate with the churn label.
heatmap corr() point-biserial VIF check
Watch out: Connection_Net and Total_Net_Paid may be highly correlated — keep only one
6
Outlier detection
Find extreme values that can distort your model. A single customer paying 100x the normal amount can skew everything. Decide whether to cap (winsorize), remove, or keep outliers.
IQR method Z-score boxplot winsorize
Focus on: Connection_Net, Total_Net_Paid, Discount_Amount, sentiment scores
7
Cross-dataset patterns (specific to your project)
Since you have 4 datasets, check how they connect. How many customers in billings have renewal calls? How many have emails? Are there customers with no calls at all? This tells you where data is sparse.
merge counts Venn overlap churn rate by data availability
e.g. Do customers with zero renewal calls churn more? That itself is a signal.

Steps 1→3 are about understanding each column on its own — you're building familiarity with the data before making comparisons. Step 4 is where the real insight happens — once you split every feature by churned vs not-churned, patterns jump out immediately. Steps 5 and 6 are about cleaning up before modelling — removing redundant or extreme features. Step 7 is unique to your multi-dataset project and very important to do before you merge. A practical tip: for step 4, a simple trick is to calculate the churn rate within each category or bin. For example, group Tenure_Years into buckets (0–1, 1–3, 3–5, 5+) and calculate the churn % in each bucket. If you see churn dropping as tenure increases, that's a clean visual story and confirms tenure is a useful feature.
